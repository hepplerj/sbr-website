In the summer of 1979, the Nevada legislature passed Assembly Bill 413, asserting state ownership over roughly 48 million acres of land managed by the federal Bureau of Land Management. The bill had no immediate legal force — Congress had not granted the land, and a lone state statute could not take it — but it crystallized a regional political movement that came to be known as the Sagebrush Rebellion.[^1]

But 1979 was neither the first nor the last such uprising. Resistance to federal land authority has surged across the American West in waves — the grazing controversies that followed the Taylor Grazing Act of 1934, the postwar fights over national-forest expansion, the Sagebrush Rebellion proper at the turn of the 1980s, the county-supremacy movement and the Wise Use coalition of the 1990s, and the armed standoffs at Bundy Ranch in 2014 and Malheur in 2016. This project treats them as a family of related conflicts — *sagebrush rebellions*, plural and lowercase — that share a constitutional argument, overlapping coalitions, and a geography concentrated along the federal-land checkerboard.

The project traces these rebellions' long arc, from the grazing politics of the New Deal through the privatization debates of the Reagan era and into the county-supremacy disputes of the 1990s, the Wise Use movement, and the armed standoffs at Bundy Ranch (2014) and Malheur (2016). It is a history of land, but also of the institutions and coalitions that fought over who got to decide what the land was *for*.

## A coalition, not a movement

The Sagebrush Rebellion is often remembered as a rancher's uprising. It was that, but it was also a coalition — one that drew together livestock associations, mining interests, timber companies, county commissioners, conservative legal foundations, and a network of think tanks that were only then beginning to find their footing in Washington.

## A geography of grievance

The grievances were not evenly distributed. They clustered along the federal land checkerboard of the Intermountain West — Nevada above all, but also eastern Oregon, southern Idaho, western Utah, and parts of Wyoming and Arizona. The map of rebellion looked very much like the map of federal ownership.

## What this project is

This is a working digital-history project. The narrative will grow as chapters are drafted; the maps and network visualizations will grow as data is cleaned and published. Everything here is versioned on [GitHub](https://github.com/hepplerj/sagebrush), and corrections are welcome.
