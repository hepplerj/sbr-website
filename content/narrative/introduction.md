In the summer of 1979, the Nevada legislature passed Assembly Bill 413 asserting state ownership over roughly 48 million acres of land managed by the federal Bureau of Land Management (BLM). Nevada rested its legal challenge to the public lands on a theory of public trust: that the government was holding the lands in trust for the state but had no actual ownership or property right to these lands. While the legislative effort eventually stalled out, it crystallized a regional political movement that came to be known as the Sagebrush Rebellion.[^1]

But 1979 was neither the first nor the last such uprising. Resistance to federal land authority is a common feature of the American West's politics---the grazing controversies that followed the Taylor Grazing Act of 1934, the postwar fights over National Forest expansion, the Sagebrush Rebellion in the 1980s, the county-supremacy movement and the Wise Use coalition of the 1990s, and the armed standoffs at Bundy Ranch in 2014 and Malheur in 2016. This project seeks to explore this long history through related debates and conflicts through coalitions, legislators, and people. 

## *The* Sagebrush Rebellion

We can center the story of the Sagebrush Rebellion in Nevada. In 1964 Congress established the Public Land Law Review Commission under Public Law 88-606 to evaluate the federal lands for either their continued management or their disposal "in a manner to provide the maximum benefit for the general public."[^2]

[^2]: Public Law 88-606, [p. 982](https://uscode.house.gov/statviewer.htm?volume=78&page=982).

The results of the PLLRC, however, dismayed western legislators. Rather than seek large-scale disposal of land, the 1970 report issued by PLLRC recommended against further land disposal and suggested a repeal of most land disposal laws still on the books in the US Code. Furthermore, PLLRC recommended new Congressional guidelines to protect environmental quality. 

Nevada organized two legislative responses: its own State Committee on Federal Land Laws to advocate for its land interests, and in 1975 another commission to "study the several possible approaches to the problem of securing a greater degree of *control* by the State of Nevada over the public lands within its borders for the common benefit of its citizens."

But the Federal Land Policy and Management Act (FLPMA) passed in 1976 upended Nevada's efforts. FLPMA provided the Bureau of Land Management the authority to manage lands for multiple-use, to "protect the quality of scientific, scenic, historical, ecological, environmental, air and atmospheric, water resources, archaeological values" of the nation. FLPMA expanded BLM's regulatory authority that seemed to privilege the interests of environmentalists and scientists over ranchers and those with financial incentives tied to federal lands.[^3]

[^3]: Skillen, *This Land is My Land*, 61-63.

Nevada intensively lobbied Congress in the intervening two years but in 1979 Nevada legislators took it even further. Two Elko county ranchers, Dean Rhodes and Norm Glaser, introduced legislation asserting Nevada's control over all unreserved federal lands in the state. The legislation spread beyond Nevada: Arizona, Colorado, Idaho, Alaska, and California all introduced similar legislation or undertook similar legislative studies of federal lands.

Nevada never fully tested the legal arguments of its legislation, even as supporters in Washington DC---Orrin Hatch and James Santini in particular---introduced congressional legislation designed to transfer federal land to state management. But the rebels were buoyed by the election of Ronald Reagan and the nomination of Secretary of the Interior James Watt, a long-time advocate for transferring public lands to the states.

The Sagebrush Rebellion exerted the political pressure necessary to enact change: not the actual transfer of land, but a more accommodating federal government. Watt had cut his teeth with the Mountain States Legal Foundation that sought to protect private property rights including lawsuits against the very government agencies he was now overseeing. Watt immediately issued a grazing fee moratorium, scrapped plans for ecological inventories of public lands, and increased leasing for oil, gas, and coal development.

## What this project is

My project seeks to understand the political ecology around the Sagebrush Rebellion, not only in the place where we see it originate but its effects beyond the Great Basin. My particular emphasis is on the Northern Plains, but the Rebellion makes for a good investigation throughout the American West. My interest on the Northern Plains---aside from being a person of the Plains---stems from a seminar paper I wrote several years ago for my mentor, Professor John Wunder. In this graduate seminar I examined the Rebellion in South Dakota---one of the only states on the Great Plains to try and enact similar Nevada-style legislation, a fact I found very puzzling. I was unsatisfied with my conclusions in the paper at the time and remain so today; it's been in the back of my mind for the last decade. So I've decided to come back to this project.

This is an in-progress digital history project. The narrative section and visualizations will grow as pieces are drafted; the maps and network visualizations will grow as data is cleaned and published. Everything here is versioned on [GitHub](https://github.com/hepplerj/sbr-website). And as I have for the last several history projects I've undertaken, I'll do much of the work here in public sharing what I can either through digital history or through narratives and essays: part open research, part public writing, part research diary. I hope you'll find it useful.
