In the summer of 1979, the Nevada legislature passed Assembly Bill 413, asserting state ownership over roughly 48 million acres of land managed by the federal Bureau of Land Management (BLM). The bill had no immediate legal force---Congress had not granted the land, and a lone state statute could not take it---but it crystallized a regional political movement that came to be known as the Sagebrush Rebellion.[^1]

But 1979 was neither the first nor the last such uprising. Resistance to federal land authority has surged across the American West in waves---the grazing controversies that followed the Taylor Grazing Act of 1934, the postwar fights over National Forest expansion, the Sagebrush Rebellion in the 1980s, the county-supremacy movement and the Wise Use coalition of the 1990s, and the armed standoffs at Bundy Ranch in 2014 and Malheur in 2016. This project seeks to explore this long history through related debates and conflicts through coalitions, legislators, and people. 

## What this project is

This is a working digital history project. The narrative section and visualizations will grow as pieces are drafted; the maps and network visualizations will grow as data is cleaned and published. Everything here is versioned on [GitHub](https://github.com/hepplerj/sbr-website).

[^1]: Nevada Assembly Bill 413, 60th Session (1979). On the movement's intellectual genealogy, see R. McGreggor Cawley, *Federal Land, Western Anger* (University Press of Kansas, 1993), esp. chap. 3.
