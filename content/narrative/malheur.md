On January 2, 2016, a group led by Ammon Bundy — son of Cliven Bundy, whose 2014 standoff with the Bureau of Land Management in Bunkerville, Nevada, had become a rallying point for armed anti-federal activism — occupied the headquarters of the Malheur National Wildlife Refuge. The stated grievance was the resentencing of two local ranchers, Dwight and Steven Hammond, under federal arson statutes. The actual argument was older: that the federal government had no constitutional claim to the land at all.

## A familiar coalition

The Malheur occupiers were not the Sagebrush Rebellion of 1979 — they were armed, they were younger, and their politics had been reshaped by two decades of Wise Use organizing, the militia movement of the 1990s, and the post-9/11 patriot networks. But the constitutional theory they invoked — that the federal government's retained public domain is illegitimate — ran in a direct line from Nevada AB 413.
