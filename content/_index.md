---
title: "Governing Ground"
description: "A digital history of the sagebrush rebellions---recurring fights over land, power, and federal authority in the American West from the 1930s through 2016."
---

Across the Forest Service grasslands of the Northern Plains, grazing allotments, energy extraction leases, and in-holdings created a patchwork of competing claims that shaped not only land use but the lives of both privileged and marginalized communities. The Sagebrush Rebellion was never just about who owned the land. It was about what the land was for, and who got to decide.

Studies of the relationship between the federal government and western rangelands once received extensive attention from historians and have experienced a slight revival among a new generation of scholars. Public land historians like Paul Wallace Gates and Vernon Carsten developed a consensus on land policy during the 1950s and 1960s that highlighted the contradictions in land policies. Gates' 1968 landmark study for the Congressional Public Land Law Review Commission *History of Public Land Law Development* concluded that public ownership of parks, forests, and dams displaced earlier American land policies of putting public land in private hands. The voices behind public ownership, multiple use, and environmental protection, however, chipped away at the federal land management consensus as users of the land---agriculturalists, recreationalists, conservationists, hunters, anglers---contested its various uses. This prompted Richard White to conclude at the dawn of the new millennium that "it is time to take another look at the public lands."
