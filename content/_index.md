---
title: "Governing Ground"
description: "A history of the sagebrush rebellions---recurring fights over land, power, and federal authority in the American West from the 1930s through 2016."
---

Across the public lands in the American West and Great Plains, grazing allotments, energy extraction leases, and in-holdings created a patchwork of competing property claims that shaped not only land use but the lives of both privileged and marginalized communities. The Sagebrush Rebellion was never just about who owned the land. It was about what the land was for, and who got to decide. 

This [project](/about/) takes up the history of these land use conflicts. While the most well-known of these is the 1979 Sagebrush Rebellion---launched in the wake of Nevada's passage of legislation that sought to assert state management over federal lands---there are many such flashpoints at different times.

Studies of the relationship between the federal government and western rangelands once received extensive attention from historians. Public land historians like Paul Wallace Gates and Vernon Carsten developed a consensus on land policy during the 1950s and 1960s that highlighted the contradictions in land policies. But starting in the 1970s the voices behind public ownership, multiple use, and environmental protection have reshaped conversations about federal land management as users of the land---agriculturalists, recreationalists, conservationists, hunters, anglers, energy companies---have contested its various uses. This prompted Richard White to conclude at the dawn of the new millennium that "it is time to take another look at the public lands."
